    Master's thesis

    Anxiety and Critical Care Needs of the Families in the ICU (unpublished)

      [Details]
      Purpose: The purpose of this study was to measure the anxiety of intensive care unit (ICU) families, measure the level to which their needs are met satisfactorily, and analyze the factors that influence their anxiety. Methods: This cross-sectional descriptive correlational study was conducted from July to November 2018 in the adult ICUs of a large tertiary hospital in Seoul, South Korea. A convenience sample of 92 family members of ICU patients who were hospitalized for more than 48 hours completed a questionnaire regarding their anxiety and their satisfaction with how their needs were met. Results: The overall mean score for the satisfaction of ICU families was 2.47 (SD = 0.56). The mean scores for the information needs scored 2.67 (SD = 0.621). Moreover, anxiety of ICU families was reported to be high, with a mean of 57.84 (SD = 13.542). The satisfaction of information needs reduced the risk of anxiety (OR=0.380, p=.027), while the number of devices being used by a patient increased the risk of anxiety (OR=1.421, p=.033). Conclusion: The findings of this study indicated that satisfying the information needs of the ICU family may lower the risk of anxiety supporting nursing interventions that target their need for information may be effective. Also, because the more devices used by the patient, the higher the risk of anxiety; the ICU family whose patient had more devices should be treated precisely to lower the risk of anxiety.
